The thesis resolves on one question: does the next wave of data-center contracts land before the Calpine debt and the guidance overhang finish resetting the multiple?

The Bull's Story
"The market priced CEG like a chip stock, panicked, and is now re-pricing it like a boring utility. It is neither. It owns the only assets that can power AI at scale — and it just got 30% bigger and 20% more profitable while the stock fell."
  • 1
    Largest U.S. nuclear fleet — physically irreplaceable
    You cannot build a new nuclear plant in a contract cycle. CEG already owns the carbon-free baseload hyperscalers are scrambling for. Scarcity is the moat. Every new AI data center is a buyer with no real alternative supplier at this scale.
  • 2
    20-year offtakes turn power into bond-like cash flow
    Microsoft (Crane/TMI, 835 MW) and Meta (Clinton, 1,121 MW) signed two-decade PPAs. That converts a commodity into contracted, inflation-linked revenue. As more capacity gets locked under long contracts, the cash flow deserves a premium multiple, not a utility discount.
  • 3
    Calpine made it accretive and coast-to-coast
    ~$2/share of accretion and ~20% earnings growth target on the deal. CEG now serves customers nationwide with both clean baseload and dispatchable gas — the exact combination data centers need for 24/7 reliability. Scale compounds the contract pipeline.
  • 4
    The de-rate is sentiment, not fundamentals
    Q1 EPS rose to $2.74 and full-year guidance of $11–$12 was reaffirmed even as the stock fell ~30% from highs. When price falls while earnings rise, the multiple is doing the work. That gap closes when one new hyperscaler deal reminds the market what it owns.
  • 5
    TMI restart is a dated, funded catalyst
    ~$1.6B to restart Crane Clean Energy Center, targeting 2028, with 45Y clean-energy tax-credit eligibility. Each regulatory milestone de-risks 835 MW of contracted Microsoft revenue. The market is paying nothing for an event with a known date and a signed buyer.

The Bear's Story
"CEG is a great utility that got mistaken for a tech stock. The correction isn't over. You're not buying a moat at a discount — you're buying a momentum unwind that still has utility-multiple gravity pulling it down, with a balance sheet that just got a lot heavier."
  • 1
    Good news no longer moves the stock — that's the tell
    A Q1 double-beat produced a 6% drop. When the market sells beats, the marginal buyer is exhausted. The de-rate from $412 isn't an overreaction to fix — it's the market correctly removing a premium it should never have paid for a regulated-asset business.
  • 2
    Calpine added ~$12.7B of debt into a higher-rate world
    Net purchase price ~$26.6B. The leverage that supercharged accretion also amplifies refinancing and rate risk. A $5B divestiture to manage the balance sheet says management feels the weight. Levered utilities don't get growth multiples.
  • 3
    The data-center contract cadence has stalled
    The whole bull case rests on a steady drumbeat of new hyperscaler PPAs. That drumbeat went quiet in 2026. Without a new signed deal, there is no catalyst to reset the bid — and the stock keeps drifting toward the low end of its range.
  • 4
    Guidance midpoint disappointed and analysts are cutting
    Argus cut fair value from $425 to $350 explicitly to align CEG with utility, not semiconductor, valuations. When the sell-side anchor moves toward utility math, the multiple compression has a floor far below where momentum traders bought.
  • 5
    Regulatory and PJM risk is unpriced optimism
    The TMI restart and behind-the-meter data-center hookups depend on PJM grid rules and regulators that haven't fully ruled. A 2028 restart is three years of execution and approval risk. The bull treats it as money in the bank; it isn't yet.

CEG is the rare fight where both corners are telling the truth. The bull owns the assets; the bear owns the tape. The company got genuinely bigger, more profitable, and more strategically central to AI — and the stock fell a third anyway. That only resolves one of two ways: the market was wrong and a new contract snaps it back, or the market is still right and "utility multiple" has another leg down. The next hyperscaler PPA, or its absence, decides which.
